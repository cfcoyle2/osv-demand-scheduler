## Sparta EV Run Strategy Manager Brief

### Purpose
Align on the most practical EV run strategy to support Sparta demand while minimizing logistics operating cost and avoiding a permanent addition of an OSV where possible.

### Decision Context
- Sparta adds new diesel and logistics demand into the Gulf of America EV run schedule.
- Current cost benchmarks are approximately $25k/day for an FSV run and $80k/day for an OSV run.
- The objective is to absorb the new demand with the existing fleet whenever operationally feasible.
- The workbook evaluated Current State plus three scheduling options.

### Executive Summary
Option 3 is the best operating strategy for most of the year because it supports Sparta without adding a dedicated OSV and keeps the system at a 5-vessel pattern instead of expanding to 6 vessels. It is the lowest-cost workable option, but it has tighter recovery margins once fronts begin rolling in. Option 1 is the lowest-risk operating plan because it separates Sparta demand onto an OSV and preserves more schedule resilience, but it materially increases cost. Option 2 should not be pursued because it overcommits FSV capacity and takes too many vessels out of the system at the same time.

### Option Assessment
**Option 1: 12-month solution with added OSV**
- Operationally stable and easiest to recover after delays.
- Mars is the only asset requiring an EV date change.
- Peak offshore pattern remains manageable and simultaneous asset-call intensity is reduced.
- Main drawback is cost: adding an OSV materially increases annual logistics spend and works against the objective of solving the demand with the existing fleet.

**Option 2: No EV date changes**
- Preserves current EV timing for all assets.
- Avoids schedule disruption on paper.
- Main drawback is fleet saturation: too many FSVs are committed at once, reducing operating flexibility and making disruption recovery difficult.
- This is not a viable execution strategy.

**Option 3: Best fit for about 8 months of the year**
- Best balance of cost and operability in normal weather conditions.
- Avoids adding a dedicated OSV and keeps the fleet footprint closer to the current model.
- Supports Sparta demand while alternating FSV and OSV coverage at Auger and ESA.
- Main drawback is reduced schedule slack. Once fronts begin, the plan becomes harder to recover because more work is absorbed into the existing fleet and recovery windows tighten.

### Recommendation
Adopt Option 3 as the base strategy for the non-front season and align now on a contingency trigger to shift into an Option 1 style support model during the higher-risk weather window. This preserves the lowest-cost workable plan for most of the year while providing a defined fallback before reliability degrades.

### Proposed Decision Statement
Approve Option 3 as the primary execution strategy for Sparta support during stable operating months, with a pre-agreed seasonal or performance-based trigger to move to supplemental OSV support if recovery risk increases.

### Suggested Trigger Points
- One missed Sparta fuel or diesel delivery window.
- Two consecutive weather-driven EV schedule slips.
- Inability to recover Glenn or the swing FSV back to planned port timing within one cycle.
- Evidence that fronts are causing repeat disruption to Auger, ESA, or Sparta rotation.

### Manager Takeaway
If the priority is lowest total logistics cost, Option 3 is the right base plan. If the priority is maximum reliability across all 12 months, Option 1 is the safer plan. The best balanced strategy is to run Option 3 by default and escalate to supplemental OSV support only when weather or recovery performance justifies the additional cost.
